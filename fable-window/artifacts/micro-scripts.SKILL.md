# Micro-Scripts: How to Write Copy That Gets Repeated

A Micro-Script is a short phrase (~5-10 words) that people *like* to repeat word-for-word — to themselves and to others. It's a story bite that compresses a brand or message into a form the brain wants to remember and pass along by word of mouth.

Examples that did real-world work:
- *Melts in your mouth, not in your hand* (M&Ms)
- *Takes a lickin' and keeps on tickin'* (Timex)
- *A diamond is forever* (De Beers)
- *Friends don't let friends drive drunk*
- *If the glove doesn't fit, you must acquit* (Cochran, OJ trial)
- *We're going to put a man on the moon by the end of the decade* (Kennedy)

The unit of selling in 2026 is no longer the 30-second spot or the LP — it's the 5-second phrase a buyer can repeat to a friend.

## When to invoke this skill

**Primary mode** — the deliverable is the phrase: taglines, slogans, headlines, brand and product names, mission and vision statements, elevator pitches, testimonial compression, founder lines, captions, DM scripts, comment-pinned answers, social posts that aim to be more than chatter, and any compress-this / make-it-stickier request. Use the Working Format at the bottom.

**Companion mode** — a host skill (`dr-script`, `shameless-script`, `email-copy`, `landing-page-copy`) owns the deliverable and pulls this framework in for hook, H1, or subject-line craft. In companion mode, apply the rules, templates, and kill-tests silently; the host skill's output contract wins. Do NOT print the Working Format (DSI statement, candidate list, template markings) inside a script, email, or LP hand-off.

## The 4½ Big Rules

Every output gets tested against these.

### Rule 1: It's not what people *hear*, it's what they *repeat*

What lands isn't what you said — it's what they want to repeat after they heard you. They'll repeat it only if they like saying it, gain something by saying it, can remember it, and it fits the story already running in their head.

### Rule 2: Every screen is a Word of Mouth Machine

Smartphones and social platforms are word-of-mouth amplifiers. WOM flips the trust sequence: with one-way media, message comes first and trust accumulates after. With WOM, trust comes from a personal endorsement *before* the transaction. People trust their tribe — friends, family, their own eyes.

**Operating principle**: *If your message doesn't work by Word of Mouth, it doesn't work.*

### Rule 3: Story isn't everything. It's the *only* thing

Stories activate the same brain regions as lived experience. The catch in 2026: you have to be able to tell your story *in about a sentence or less*.

### Rule 4: Give 'em a Micro-Script

Compress the story to its repeatable bite. Use metaphors, vivid imagery, rhythmic cadence, rhyme. Make the phrase the audience's "self-talk" — the line they'll think to themselves and say to others.

### Rule 4½: Build it on a Dominant Selling Idea (DSI)

Random clever phrases sell nothing. A marketing Micro-Script must be built on a **DSI** — your single, superlative, important, believable, measurable, own-able difference. (DSI = positioning by another name.) Get the DSI first, then the story, then the Micro-Script.

A DSI passes five tests:
1. **Superlative** — what you are #1 at (safest, fastest, longest-lasting, only-one-with)
2. **Important** — matters to the customer; a must-have, not a nice-to-have
3. **Believable** — credible reason-to-believe behind the claim
4. **Measurable** — provable in performance, not just talk
5. **Own-able** — not already taken by a competitor

## The Four Templates

Most Micro-Scripts run on one of four structures. Use them explicitly when drafting.

| Template | Pattern | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **A/B Equation** | A is B, A causes B, If A then B, A vs B, A saves B | *Honesty is the best policy*; *No pain, no gain*; *What goes around comes around*; *A diamond is forever* |
| **Stark Reminder** | "Do this" / "Don't do that," period | *Stop, drop, and roll*; *Just do it*; *Save the whales*; *Just say no* |
| **Unique Wordplay** | Distinctive word construction with rhythm/rhyme | *Snap, Crackle, and Pop*; *Mmmm, mmmm, good*; *Woulda, coulda, shoulda* |
| **Whole Micro-Story** | Compresses a story arc into a phrase | *For sale: baby shoes, never worn*; *He furloughed the murderer*; *Texas Chainsaw Massacre* |

The strongest Micro-Scripts combine 2+ templates plus key ingredients.

## Key Ingredients

Layer at least one or two of these into every candidate:

1. **Word patterns, rhythm, rhyme** — alliteration, assonance, repetitive sequence, meter. *Click it or ticket*. *When in doubt, throw it out*. *Curiosity killed the cat*.
2. **Vivid, colorful language** — show, don't tell. Strong verbs, sensory words. Avoid corporate words: *passion, dedication, excellence, deliver, leverage, enhance, optimize, facilitate*. They never make it into Micro-Scripts.
3. **Simple metaphors** — "A is B." Metaphors hypnotize the right brain into forming visualizations the audience fills in themselves. *Life is like a box of chocolates*.

## The Process: Hypothesize → Memorialize → Synthesize → Revise

Use this when generating fresh copy from a brief or business problem. Scale it to the ask: a full brand-story pass for naming/positioning work; a compressed version (DSI in one line, straight to candidates) for a quick tagline or headline batch.

### 1. Hypothesize

Ask Master Aligning Questions of stakeholders (or yourself, if it's a self-brief). Use open questions (*what, why, who, when, where, how*), not yes/no. Sample questions:

- What is your category? Does it have a name?
- Who are your main competitors? What are they famous for?
- What are your *onlies* — what do you do that no one else does?
- What do you want to be famous for?
- "We're the #1 choice for ___ because we're the only ones who ___"
- Is there an unfilled need — a hole in the category?
- Why will you win? Why could you lose?

Salespeople and customer-service reps are gold — they're already speaking the customer's language under live conditions. Borrow their phrasing.

### 2. Memorialize

Write a **one-page brand story**. Before-and-after structure: once the world was like this, but there was a problem, so [the brand] came along, did this, this, and this, and now the world is better. Make the DSI the star.

The act of writing the story warms up the metaphor/imagery machinery. **The best headline is always in the copy** — old Ted Bates rule. Your Micro-Scripts will surface inside the prose.

### 3. Synthesize

Pull the phrases that read like Micro-Scripts out of the story. Aim for 5-6 candidates. Often they'll snap together into a complete Micro-Pitch (elevator speech).

### 4. Revise

Try them out in real conversations and channels. The ones customers repeat back are the real Micro-Scripts. The ones they don't, discard. **Listening is itself a strategy** — customers will hand you better language than you'd invent. (Airborne's "take it before you get on a plane" came from customers, not the company.)

## Candidate gauntlet — ordered checklist with kill-criteria

Run every candidate phrase through these, in this order. The first two are hard kills: fail either and the candidate dies regardless of the rest.

1. **DSI test (HARD KILL).** Built on a superlative + important + believable + measurable + own-able difference? A clever line with no DSI under it is decoration; kill it before polishing.
2. **Repeat test (HARD KILL).** Would a real customer want to say this to another customer, out loud, verbatim? If you can't picture the sentence coming out of a buyer's mouth, kill it.
3. **WOM test.** Does it work standing alone, or does it need 30 seconds of context? KILL: context-dependent lines.
4. **Story-bite test.** Does it tell, trigger, or fragment a story? KILL: pure assertions — they don't compound.
5. **Length test.** Under ~10 words? KILL: anything longer that can't be pruned without losing the story.
6. **Template test.** A/B Equation, Stark Reminder, Unique Wordplay, or Whole Micro-Story? KILL: shapeless lines — restructure onto a template or discard.
7. **Ingredient test.** Rhythm/rhyme, vivid language, or metaphor? At least one; two is better. KILL: flat lines with none.
8. **Vocabulary sweep.** KILL: any corporate word (*passion, dedication, excellence, deliver, leverage, enhance, optimize, synergy, solutions, world-class*) and any em dash.

**Ship bar:** a candidate ships only if it survives both hard kills and at least 4 of the remaining 6 tests, AND you can name its template and its ingredient. If fewer than 5 of your candidates survive, generate more — do not pad the list with the dead.

## Anti-Patterns (Things That Kill Micro-Scripts)

- **Generic taglines** — *A Passion for Possible*, *Defend What's Yours*, *Now That's Eating*. Could belong to any company. Payoff: nothing.
- **Empty mission statements** — paragraphs of "relentless focus on imperatives" that no employee can repeat.
- **Pretty branding without a DSI** — logo, color, typography work without an idea-attached-to-the-name underneath.
- **Awareness as the goal** — awareness is half the battle. Word-of-mouth-able preference is the other half.
- **Headlines that don't work as standalone selling tools** — 85% of readers never read body copy. The headline must do the selling alone.
- **Corporate vocabulary** — *passion, dedication, excellence, deliver, leverage, enhance, optimize, synergy, solutions, world-class*. Replace every one with a sensory or specific word.

## Failure modes — do not repeat

- **Clever-first drafting.** Generating wordplay before the DSI is stated. Every session starts by writing the DSI in one sentence; candidates come after.
- **Working Format leakage.** Printing the DSI statement, template markings, and candidate scoring inside a script/email/LP hand-off when running in companion mode. The host skill's plaintext contract wins; the gauntlet runs silently.
- **Process theater.** Running the full Hypothesize → Memorialize pass for a quick "give me a tagline" ask. Compress the process to fit the ask; don't ship a brand-story essay nobody requested.
- **Padding the shortlist.** Keeping candidates that failed the gauntlet to hit "5 candidates". Generate more instead; a list of 5 with 2 dead lines reads as 5 recommendations.
- **Scoring your own cleverness.** Passing a line on the repeat test because *you* like it. The test is a buyer saying it to a friend, verbatim, unprompted.
- **Essay defense.** Refuting a hostile Micro-Script with paragraphs. Match the weapon class (see below).
- **Corporate leak.** A banned corporate word or an em dash surviving into a shipped candidate. The vocabulary sweep is the last gate before printing.

## Naming as a Micro-Script Function

A great brand or product name *is* a Micro-Script. Four tests:

1. Direct statement of, or highly supportive of, the DSI
2. Descriptive, evocative, or colorful in some way
3. Own-able by you
4. Easy or pleasing to repeat

Good examples: *Diehard Batteries*, *Invisible Fence*, *Five-Hour Energy*, *Head and Shoulders*, *The Cheesecake Factory*, *Gentle Dental*, *Egg Beaters*, *RollerBlade*.

Anti-patterns: *Sentient* (renamed from eBizJets — meaningless, gives up the category fight); initial-jockeys (*CVX, GDW, CYA*) without decades of brand equity to redeem them; "Richard Dick" trap (don't pick a name whose nickname you'll hate).

### Name + Tag Combo

Pair a Micro-Scripted name with a Micro-Scripted tagline to get a one-line elevator pitch. *Diehard Batteries. Never get stuck again*. *Kashi: 7 Whole Grains on a Mission*.

## The Micro-Pitch (Elevator Pitch)

Build by stringing 5-6 of your synthesized Micro-Scripts together with connecting words. Should fit in ~30 seconds. Example structure:

- Open with the category + #1 claim
- 2-3 lines on the proof / difference
- Close with the emotional payoff or the call

Ideal end-state: compress the whole pitch to a single sentence. *"Made from sugar, so it tastes like sugar."* (Splenda.) That's the entire pitch in 8 words.

## Mission and Vision Statements in Micro

If you can't say the mission in one or two lines, you can't say it. Examples that pass:

- *"We're going to put a man on the moon by the end of the decade."*
- *"We're going to fix the way the world sits."* (BackJoy)
- *"Fanatical Support."* (Rackspace)
- *"It's not TV, it's HBO."*

Strip every adjective that isn't doing load-bearing work.

## Testimonials in Micro

Long testimonials are dead. No one reads paragraphs of "Hooli has been really great for our business" anymore. Compress to a story bite (~10 words):

- *"We got 50% more calls the first day!"*
- *"At 350 mph, these sunglasses saved my life."*

Take any verbatim testimonial and reduce to its dramatic essence. The customer doesn't care if you compress — keep the meaning intact.

## Defending Against Hostile Micro-Scripts

When a Micro-Script is used against you (*flip-flopper*, *crooked Hillary*, *low-energy Jeb*), denial reinforces the attack. Two effective responses:

1. **Catch the grenade and throw it back.** Acknowledge, reframe, then label the attacker with the same charge.
2. **Deflect with a stronger Micro-Script.** *"Those who never change their minds never change anything."* — Churchill.

Cardinal rule: never refute a Micro-Script with an essay. Match the weapon class.

## Working Format When Producing Output

When this skill is active **in primary mode**, structure copy deliverables like this:

1. **State the DSI** in one sentence (category + superlative + reason-to-believe).
2. **List 5 Micro-Script candidates** that pass the gauntlet above.
3. **Mark which template + ingredients** each one uses.
4. **Pick the strongest 1-2** and explain why.
5. **If asked for full copy** (script, LP, email), build the body around the chosen Micro-Script — every section reinforces it, never restates it differently each time.

In companion mode, skip this format entirely and deliver through the host skill's contract.
