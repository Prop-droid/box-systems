## Response Templates

### 1. Price / Value

**Customer phrasing:** "Too expensive," "$3.50 a bag is steep," "I can get Haribo for less."

**Reframe:** cost-per-fiber-gram, subscription math, replacement for fiber supplement + candy simultaneously.

**Ad / LP:** "26g fiber per bag. Your fiber supplement is $0.80 a serving — and tastes like sand. This is both."
**Comment reply:** Subscribe & Save brings it to $2.69/bag (33% off) — that's less than a bottle of kombucha, with 90% of your daily fiber.
**DM:** If price is the blocker, Subscribe & Save locks $2.69/bag. You can cancel anytime after your first order.

### 2. Digestive Side Effects

**Customer phrasing:** "Had me running to the bathroom," "fiber bomb," "too much too fast."

**Reframe:** fiber is doing its job; start with half a bag if new to high-fiber; hydrate.

**Ad / LP:** Not a lead angle — addressed in FAQ only. Never lead creative with digestive disruption.
**Comment reply:** Totally fair — 26g of fiber is more than most people get in a day. We recommend easing in with half a bag for the first few days if you're not used to high-fiber foods. And drink water.
**DM:** Same as above.
**Compliance flag:** Never frame as "detox" or "cleanse" — that triggers disease-claim territory ([[compliance-guardrails]]).

### 3. Sweeteners / Sugar Alcohols

**Customer phrasing:** "Is this allulose?" "Does it have sugar alcohols?" "I can't do stevia." "Would rather have monk fruit or stevia." "Polydextrose is a synthetic material too."

**Reframe:** Uses erythritol, isomalt, sucralose — see [[product-facts]] for exact formulation. No added refined sugar. Competitor [[competitive-landscape|SmartSweets]] uses allulose + stevia; some consumers prefer plant-derived.

**Ad / LP:** Stat-led comparison on fiber, not on sweetener type (we lose that fight vs. allulose brands).
**Comment reply:** Sweetened with a blend that keeps sugar at 3g and calories at 70 — full ingredient list at [URL]. Happy to share specifics if you've got a particular restriction.
**DM:** Same.
**Compliance:** Never say "no artificial sweeteners" (sucralose is present). See [[compliance-guardrails]].

### 4. Customer Service / Shipping

**Customer phrasing:** "Order hasn't shipped," "subscription charged me," "can't cancel." "Added things I didn't purchase and charged me for it."

**Sub-type — Unauthorized charges:** Distinct from subscription-confusion complaints. Customers report items added to orders they didn't authorize. Treat as P0 escalation — this is a billing integrity issue beyond the standard "can't cancel" pattern.

**Compliance-critical:** FTC subscription complaints are a pattern risk. See [[checkout-funnel]] trust-signals audit.

**Comment reply (DM escalation):** I'm sorry about that — can you DM us your order number? We'll get this sorted today.
**DM:** Resolve directly. Do not argue publicly.

### 5. Flavor Consistency

**Customer phrasing:** "Loved peach, hated sour," "the new flavors aren't as good."

**Reframe:** flavor variety is a feature; variety packs; subscribe to rotate.

**Comment reply:** Totally fair — flavor is personal. Variety packs let you sample before committing to a favorite.

### 6. Bag Size

**Customer phrasing:** "Bag is smaller than I expected," "only lasts me two sittings."

**Reframe:** serving size is the whole bag by design; compare to candy serving sizes.

**Ad / LP:** Permission frame — "the whole bag is the serving size." Avoid defensive framing.

### 7. Skepticism ("Can't Taste Good")

**Customer phrasing:** "Healthy candy always tastes terrible," "this is fake candy."

**Reframe:** converted-skeptic is the single most common review pattern. Lead with it.

**Ad / LP:** Skeptic-convert hook ("I thought healthy candy was a lie. Then I tried this."). See [[hook-framework]].
**Comment reply:** Totally get it — 90% of our reviewers say the same thing before trying. OMG Peach is the usual converter.
**Static headline:** "I thought this was fake candy. Reader, I was wrong."

### 8. Safety for Diabetics

**Customer phrasing:** "Is this safe for diabetics?" "Will this spike my blood sugar?"

**Compliance-critical:** Cannot make direct medical safety claims. See [[compliance-guardrails]] Hard Stops.

**Ad / LP:** 3g sugar, 26g fiber. Let the stats speak.
**Comment reply (approved):** 3g sugar per bag and 26g fiber. We always recommend talking to your doctor about what's right for your specific situation.
**Never say:** "Safe for diabetics," "won't spike blood sugar," "diabetic-friendly."

### 9. GLP-1 / Food Noise

**Customer phrasing:** "I'm on Ozempic and constipated," "GLP-1 killed my fiber intake."

**Compliance-critical:** Never name drugs. "GLP-1" as a category is permitted in imagery/context but the script should ground in fiber mechanism. "Food noise" as a term is allowed (mainstream lifestyle usage, appears in shipped ads); keep it grounded in the fiber mechanism rather than drug framing.

**Ad / LP:** Storytelling hook, personal, fiber-routing. See [[script-leaderboard]] (Genetics, Dad Bod).
**Comment reply:** Fiber's been a game-changer for folks in similar spots — 26g per bag with 3g sugar. Always talk to your doctor.

### 10. Texture Anxiety

**Customer phrasing:** "What's the texture like?" "Are they chewy?" "Are they hard?" "Are they sticky?"

**The #1 pre-purchase question in Facebook comments.** Dozens of customers ask about chewiness before buying. Two opposing complaint clusters exist: "too soft / not chewy enough" (vs. Sour Patch Kids) and "too sticky / hard on teeth."

**Ad / LP:** A dedicated texture video showing the actual chew would reduce conversion friction significantly. Close-up of gummies, bag opening, the bite.
**Comment reply:** They're a soft, chewy gummy — closer to a fruit snack than a hard candy. Not sticky, not firm like SmartSweets. Most people say the texture is what surprised them most.
**Product team flag:** A firmer texture variant for the sour lineup could address the most vocal complaint segment.

### 11. Erythritol Safety (Cleveland Clinic Study)

**Customer phrasing:** "Erythritol causes heart attacks," "Cleveland Clinic study," "linked to blood clots."

17+ distinct comments citing the 2023 Cleveland Clinic study linking erythritol to cardiovascular risk. Customers are posting links to NIH, PubMed, and AHA journal articles directly in ad comment sections. Particularly acute for the diabetic audience ([[customer-segments|Dave]]) that comprises a significant portion of the customer base.

**Ad / LP:** Do not address proactively in creative — don't amplify the concern. Address only when asked directly.
**Comment reply:** We understand the concern. The Cleveland Clinic study looked at very high blood concentrations, not dietary intake levels. Our formulation uses erythritol as one of several sweeteners to keep sugar at 3g per bag. We always recommend discussing dietary choices with your doctor.
**Product team flag:** Reformulation toward allulose or monk fruit would address both this concern and the sweetness aftertaste issue simultaneously. [[competitive-landscape|Joyride]] already positions as erythritol-free.

### 12. Sourness Mismatch ("Are They All Sour?")

**Customer phrasing:** "Are they all sour?" "I hate sour candy — do you have regular gummies?" "Are any of these not sour?"

The single most repeated question in the entire dataset — 35+ standalone instances across 2022-2026. Represents a massive pool of potential customers blocked by sour uncertainty. Full analysis: [[flavor-strategy#The Sourness Problem]].

**Ad / LP:** Proactive flavor categorization — a "sweet vs. sour" visual guide showing which flavors are mild/sweet (Whales, Cherry Bears, Peach, Watermelon) vs. sour (Blue Raspberry, Red Raspberry, Cherry Bomb).
**Comment reply:** Not all sour! Our Wunderlicious Whales and So Beary Cherry are sweet with zero sour coating. Peach and Watermelon are mild — more tangy than sour. The Blue Raspberry and Cherry Bomb are our sourest.
**DM:** Same, plus link to the Sweet Collection (when launched).

### 13. AI-Generated Content in Ads

**Customer phrasing:** "Hold the packaging still," "this looks AI-generated," "I don't trust AI ads."

Highest-liked brand-related comment in the dataset (26 likes): "Hold the packaging still." Multiple customers report abandoning a purchase because the ad looked AI-generated. This is a trust issue — customers already skeptical of "healthy candy" claims are further put off by creative that feels artificial.

**Ad / LP:** Remove AI-generated content from all ads. Use real product photography and real UGC only.
**Comment reply:** That's real product, real person! We hear you though — we're committed to keeping it authentic.

### 14. Made in China Trust Concern

**Customer phrasing:** "Where are these made?" "Are these made in China?" "I only buy American-made."

Confirmed across 3 channels (Facebook, QVC, Amazon). A trust barrier for a subset of health-conscious buyers who associate imported food products with lower quality or safety standards.

**Comment reply:** Never claim "Made in USA" or US manufacturing — the product is manufactured abroad and packed in the US, and unqualified US-origin claims are FTC-policed. Approved framing: "Packed in the US in an FDA-registered, GMP-certified facility" (verify the facility holds the certs before using). Then redirect to ingredient quality and testing standards.

### 15. Subscription-Trap Fear (Grüns / ColonBroom legacy)

**Customer phrasing:** "Another subscription? I cancelled Grüns twice and it kept coming." / "I don't want a quiz-gate." / "How do I cancel?"

Source: competitor-research-2026-04-23.md (v1), competitor-research-deep-2026-04-23.md (v2 — Raul L. named complaint). The #1 unmet customer ask in the category: Grüns BBB F→NR (25 of 30 unresponded complaints), ColonBroom quiz-gate + scrubbed reviews. See [[competitive-landscape]] and [[transparent-operations-proof-pack]].

**Reframe:** One-click cancel. Phone-answerable. No quiz-gate. No restocking fee. No surprise early charges.

**Ad / LP:** Use the "no subscription trap" block (per P1.1 in shameless-action-backlog-2026-04-23.md). *Defensive promise framing* — not competitor attack. Soften direct Grüns/ColonBroom naming in paid Meta placements (Meta's "negative competitor claim" policy) — keep specificity for LP and organic social.
**Comment reply:** Totally hear you. Our cancel is one-click from your account page, and we've got a real phone number if you prefer. No quiz, no hoops, no restocking fee.
**DM:** If you want to cancel anytime, just reply here and we'll get it done today.

### 16. Fiber Safety on GLP-1

**Customer phrasing:** "Fiber makes my GLP-1 constipation worse — why is yours different?" / "My Reddit says don't add fiber on MJ." / "Won't this give me a bezoar?"

Source: competitor-research-deep-2026-04-23.md (v2). Peer consensus on r/Mounjaro / r/Wegovy / r/Ozempic is *"Miralax first, fiber second."* ER-visit stories are real (*"went to hospital once after 10 days of not going"*). Metamucil's homepage now claims GLP-1 suitability but peers still say *"don't."*

**Compliance-critical:** Interaction-with-Rx-class is regulated. Use Metamucil's exact HCP-consult hedge: *"Consult your healthcare provider about fiber while on your GLP-1 medication."* See [[glp1-dosing-companion]] and [[compliance-guardrails]].

**Reframe:** Shameless is candy with fiber — start low, drink water, wait. Mirror peer-vocabulary ("start with half a bag, 16 oz water, wait 90 minutes") rather than inventing a medical claim.

**Ad / LP:** Never say "safe with Ozempic/Mounjaro/Wegovy" (drug-name hard stop). Say *"On a GLP-1? Start with half a bag. Drink 16 oz. Wait 90 minutes. See how your body responds. Consult your doctor about fiber while on your GLP-1."*
**Comment reply:** Fair question — the consensus on r/Mounjaro is "start low, drink water, wait." That's literally our dosing guidance: half a bag first, 16 oz water, 90 minutes. And your doctor's call if you're already constipated — Miralax comes first.
**DM:** Same. Never prescribe. Always HCP-hedge.

### 17. Hidden Ingredients / Third-Party Scoring

**Customer phrasing:** "What does Yuka say?" / "Is there a Prop 65 warning?" / "What's in the proprietary blend?" / "I don't trust supplements with hidden ingredients."

Source: competitor-research-2026-04-23.md (v1 — Grüns Prop 65, ColonBroom undisclosed sucralose, proprietary blend critique). Yuka app scored Shameless 42/100 with 11 additives flagged (per [[competitive-landscape]] Joyride switcher note). Customers already compare via Yuka before purchase.

**Reframe:** Full ingredient disclosure. No proprietary blend. Per-batch COA (third-party heavy-metal results). See [[transparent-operations-proof-pack]].

**Ad / LP:** Full ingredient list visible above the fold. "Per-batch Certificate of Analysis" as a trust block — when live. Don't lead with sucralose-as-clean (Shameless uses it — attack sucralose-as-hidden, not sucralose-as-ingredient; see v1 open flags).
**Comment reply:** Full ingredient list is here: [link]. No proprietary blend. We publish a COA per batch when the batch ships. Sucralose is in there — disclosed on-pack — which is why we focus on fiber density and transparency, not "clean label" framing we can't back up.
**DM:** Same.

### 18. "Expensive Candy Like Grüns"

**Customer phrasing:** "Isn't this just expensive candy like Grüns?" / "$71/month for gummies is insane."

Source: competitor-research-deep-2026-04-23.md (v2). Grüns $2.14–$2.86/serving subscription vs. Shameless subscription pricing. ColonBroom $0.75–$2.33/serving for 3.6 g psyllium.

**Reframe:** Per-gram-of-fiber math. 26 g per bag is 2–8x any competitor per bag/serving. Subscribe-and-Save → $2.69/bag. See [[checkout-funnel]].

**Ad / LP:** Comparison chart — Shameless × Metamucil × Benefiber × Grüns × ColonBroom. Shameless wins on fiber density + subscription integrity + origin disclosure cleanly. See [[competitive-landscape#Updated quantitative side-by-side]].
**Comment reply:** Fair comparison. Grüns gives you 6 g per pack for ~$2.14. We give you 26 g per bag for $2.69 on subscription — more than 4x the fiber per dollar, phone-answerable cancel, no quiz-gate.
**DM:** Same plus subscribe-and-save link.

## Phrasing Not to Use

From [[compliance-guardrails]] — even if a customer says it, don't amplify:

- "Food noise" (drug-mechanism term)
- "Melt fat" / "burn fat"
- "Cured my [condition]"
- "Replaces my [drug name]"
- "Detox" / "cleanse"
- Body transformation imagery or weight-before/after
